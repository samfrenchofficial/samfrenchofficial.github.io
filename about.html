From an early age, I developed a strong affinity for hands-on problem solving through DIY projects and metal fabrication, leading me to pursue Mechanical Engineering at Cork Institute of Technology (now Munster Technological University). I graduated with First Class Honours in 2018 and later completed an Honours Degree in Mechanical Engineering in 2023, building a strong technical foundation. My approach to engineering is firmly user-centric, with a keen focus on usability, marketability, and aesthetics alongside technical performance. I gained practical industry experience through work with Crowley’s Engineering and an internship with PM Group during the Covid-19 pandemic at Novartis Ringaskiddy Limited. Beyond formal roles, I remain actively engaged in the Maker community. My interests in 3D printing, automotive engineering, and rapid prototyping allow me to continually refine my design intuition and explore emerging technologies.

Growing up in close proximity to Stryker’s Innovation Centre in Cork, the company was always a familiar presence in my life, but its mission became profoundly personal during my early teens. My twin sister, Lydia, began experiencing severe and unexplained spinal pain that gradually escalated into a life-altering condition. Years of misdiagnosis, mounting physical agony, and significant emotional strain culminated in a critical diagnosis of Scheuermann’s disease and degenerative disc disease. What followed was a period marked by uncertainty, resilience, and the sobering reality of how fragile quality of life can be when health deteriorates.

Lydia’s eventual spinal fusion surgery represented both risk and hope. Faced with limited options, our family placed its trust in a highly complex surgical intervention, supported by an array of precisely engineered instruments. Watching the procedure explained in detail, I was struck not only by the technical sophistication involved, but by the responsibility carried by every tool, design decision, and process step. Recognising the role of Stryker instruments throughout that procedure transformed my understanding of engineering—from an abstract discipline into something deeply human, tangible, and consequential.

Nearly ten years on, Lydia’s life stands as a testament to what well-designed medical technology can achieve. While she continues to live with aspects of her disability, she has regained independence, purpose, and ambition, most recently completing a Master’s degree. This experience has shaped my professional motivation at its core. It reinforces my belief that our work does not simply meet specifications or regulatory requirements—it restores dignity, capability, and futures. Every design, test, and improvement matters, because behind each product is a life waiting to be reclaimed.
